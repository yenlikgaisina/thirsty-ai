This is England.

Home to roughly 56 million people — and a water network under growing strain from population, climate change and leakage.

Much of it is already water-stressed.

The Environment Agency classifies large swathes of England — the South East, East, and parts of the Midlands and South West — as areas of serious water stress (2021 classification).

Now the data centres arrive.

Planning applications doubled in 2025. We mapped 72 proposed and planned schemes — announced, submitted, approved or under construction as of June 2026.

And they cluster where the water isn't.

79% of the England-based schemes in our register sit in officially water-stressed areas. Here's every one.

    Methodology & caveats

    Each row is a publicly reported UK data centre scheme (announced, submitted, approved or under construction as of June 2026 — none yet operational), compiled from industry trackers, the House of Commons Library, government announcements and regional press. Water-company areas in serious water stress per the Environment Agency's 2021 classification are flagged. (Severn Trent’s Chester supply zone was excluded from the 2021 designation; none of the sites in this register are near Chester, so no rows are affected.) Per-site water use is shown as a range using a per-MW band derived from the two anchor figures in the Government’s “Water use in AI and Data Centres” report (GDSA, Section 2, “Global and UK water consumption statistics” table): ~20,000 L/MW/day (an average 100 MW site using ~2 million litres/day, cooling only) up to ~68,500 L/MW/day (a 100 MW hyperscale facility using ~2.5 billion litres/year). Advanced cooling (cold-plate/immersion) can cut this 31–52% versus air cooling, and ~80% of evaporative-cooling water — often potable — is lost to evaporation. People-equivalent comparisons use the report’s own equivalence (a 100 MW hyperscale facility ≈ the daily water needs of ~80,000 people, i.e. ~800 people per MW), shown as a range. For context the report notes England’s public supply is ~14,000 Ml/day against a projected ~5,000 Ml/day deficit by 2050, and that only two-fifths of operators track their water use. The UK has no central data centre register, so this is a best-effort compilation, not exhaustive; some schemes are paused or under legal challenge (labelled per row); Scotland & Wales sit outside the EA's England-only classification and are shown as "not classified", which does not mean water-abundant.

    Industry will say "closed-loop cooling uses little water." That's true for some cooling types — which is exactly why every figure here is a range, and why the fact that 33 of 72 schemes won't disclose their cooling or power use is itself the story. This map shows where schemes are proposed and whether they sit in stressed areas; it does not claim a single confirmed water figure for any site.
